The aim of this article is to provide for the early detection of the cause of malignancy in order to prevent further loss of life and disability in patients affected in tuberculosis.

## Introduction
Several fungal species of the genus Cryptococcus are saprophytes capable of causing infections in immunocompromised patients. In addition to infections caused by C. neoformans and C. gattii, Cryptococcus gattii can cause disease in both immunocompetent and immunocompromised patients. C. gattii is endemic in the Northern Hemisphere and has a variety of environmental hosts, including humans, pigeons, and bats. In addition to the global distribution, C. gattii can also infect humans, cats, and other mammals, especially in the environment.

The global incidence of cryptococcal disease is estimated to be more than 220 cases and 600,000 cases annually, resulting in approximately 600,000 deaths annually (1). Cryptococcus neoformans and C. gattii are the two most common causes of meningoencephalitis in patients with HIV/AIDS (2-4). Clinical presentations range from asymptomatic but life-threatening cryptococcal meningoencephalitis to severe pulmonary and central nervous system (CNS) disease.
